Motivation & Research

This section explains why we chose the X·EasyGo project and how academic research and existing products informed our design direction.

The Why

Why this project matters

We chose this project because traditional campus tours often position visitors as passive listeners rather than active explorers. At XJTLU, this is an important problem because the university's student-centered philosophy cannot be fully understood through one-way explanation alone. It is better experienced through movement across classrooms, learning spaces, social areas, and campus culture. Existing tours can provide basic information, but they do not always help new students build a memorable connection with the university or help external visitors understand the educational thinking embedded in each space. This matters especially for educators, administrators, and ILEAD-related visitors, who may see the campus physically without fully grasping how XJTLU's environment supports collaboration, autonomy, and innovation.

We therefore saw an opportunity to design a campus tour that is mobile, interactive, and spatial rather than purely descriptive. Our goal is not simply to tell people about XJTLU, but to let them discover it through route-based guidance, meaningful places, and lightweight interaction. By combining location-based navigation, gamified check-ins, XR elements, and AI-supported interaction, X·EasyGo can respond more flexibly to different user groups. In this way, the campus itself becomes part of the storytelling, helping users understand both where they are and why those spaces matter. It also makes the experience feel more personal, exploratory, and connected to the real rhythm of campus life.

The Gap

What existing work does well and where it falls short

To identify opportunities for improvement, we reviewed four academic papers and four commercial products related to location-based AR, campus orientation, mobile navigation, and playful exploration. Together, they show valuable patterns for guidance, storytelling, and engagement, but they also reveal recurring gaps in usability, role-based support, and the balance between practical navigation and meaningful learning.

Academic Papers

4 papers reviewed to understand educational value, feasibility, and design risks.

Paper 01

Location-Based Augmented Reality in Education

Kleftodimos & Evagelou (2025)

Relevance: Explains how location-based AR connects learning content with real-world places.

Strengths

  • • Clearly explains the educational value of location-based AR.
  • • Describes triggering technologies such as GPS and indoor positioning.
  • • Provides practical examples from heritage and outdoor learning.

Limitations

  • • Lacks strong empirical evidence.
  • • Focuses more on technology than UX challenges.
  • • Many examples are closer to heritage learning than campus navigation.

Paper 02

Using a Location-Based AR Mobile App to Support New Students’ Orientation

Taşyonar & Uzun (2025)

Relevance: Directly studies AR-supported campus orientation for new students.

Strengths

  • • Highly relevant to campus orientation.
  • • Clearly explains implementation and system design.
  • • Includes real student testing and empirical evidence.

Limitations

  • • Did not outperform traditional guided orientation in knowledge learning.
  • • Reported technical and device compatibility issues.
  • • Small sample size limits generalisability.

Paper 03

A Game-based AR Navigation System for Makerspace Education

Chen, Hwang & Lin (2023)

Relevance: Shows how AR and gamification can support active exploration.

Strengths

  • • Combines AR with gamified missions and rewards.
  • • Provides a clear and structured interaction flow.
  • • Supports active learning through problem-solving tasks.

Limitations

  • • May prioritise engagement over learning depth.
  • • Context is limited to a library makerspace.
  • • Interaction design may be too complex for general mobile users.

Paper 04

Location-based AR in Education: A Systematic Literature Review

Fonseca et al. (2025)

Relevance: Provides a broad overview of location-based AR in educational settings.

Strengths

  • • Reviews a wide range of educational use cases.
  • • Demonstrates strong potential for engagement and experiential learning.
  • • Identifies common trends such as field trips and outdoor learning.

Limitations

  • • Many studies still lack strong experimental evidence.
  • • Limited integration into formal education.
  • • Technical and usability challenges remain significant.

Commercial Products

4 products reviewed to analyse navigation, storytelling, exploration, and interaction patterns.

Product 01

Google Maps

Why relevant: A strong reference for map-based navigation and place information.

Strengths

  • • Strong navigation and map support.
  • • Familiar interface with low learning cost.
  • • Integrates place-related information effectively.

Limitations

  • • Designed for navigation rather than guided learning.
  • • Does not provide AR narration.
  • • Does not distinguish between different user roles.

Product 02

izi.TRAVEL

Why relevant: Useful for self-paced exploration and location-based storytelling.

Strengths

  • • Supports location-based audio guidance.
  • • Encourages self-paced exploration.
  • • Combines route structure with storytelling.

Limitations

  • • More audio-focused than AR-focused.
  • • Has limited playful interaction.
  • • Weak support for differentiated user roles.

Product 03

Pokémon GO

Why relevant: Demonstrates how location-linked interaction can motivate exploration.

Strengths

  • • Demonstrates strong location-based interaction.
  • • Creates a strong sense of exploration and engagement.
  • • Blends AR-style interaction with everyday movement.

Limitations

  • • Designed for entertainment rather than education.
  • • Lacks structured explanatory content.
  • • Does not support user-specific information delivery.

Product 04

Actionbound

Why relevant: A useful model for mission-based exploration and active participation.

Strengths

  • • Supports task-based and route-based exploration.
  • • Encourages active participation.
  • • Flexible for educational content design.

Limitations

  • • Has limited AR emphasis.
  • • Depends heavily on creator setup quality.
  • • May feel more like a scavenger hunt than a guided AR tour.
Gap Summary

What existing work does well and what it still misses

3 Things They Did Well

  • • They show strong potential for combining route guidance with place-based information.
  • • They support self-paced exploration and can make users more active participants.
  • • They demonstrate that storytelling, AR, and playful interaction can increase engagement.

3 Things They Missed

  • • Many solutions are strong in immersion but weaker in fast, practical usability.
  • • Most do not clearly adapt content for different user groups such as students and visitors.
  • • Few combine campus guidance with deeper educational meaning in one coherent mobile experience.

Design Process

01

Initial Concept

The project began with the idea of improving the campus tour experience for visitors and new students. Traditional tours often rely on static maps or human guides, which may not always be available.

02

Research

Research focused on extended reality technologies, location-based interaction systems, and existing digital tour applications. The goal was to identify how XR could enhance spatial storytelling.

03

Interaction Design

Different interaction models were explored, including how users trigger content, how the AR guide appears, and how information should be presented to maintain engagement without overwhelming users.

04

Prototype Development

A prototype was developed using XR development tools. The system implemented location detection, AR visualization, and a basic virtual guide character that introduces selected landmarks.

05

Testing and Refinement

The prototype was evaluated and refined by adjusting trigger distances, improving guide narration, and optimizing interaction flow to make the experience smoother for users.

The updated questionnaire is strongly student-led, so student behaviour is the clearest evidence base in the dataset. Student Users

The strongest signal in the survey

  • • 90.2% of respondents are Year 3 students, so everyday campus movement is the dominant behaviour pattern in the sample.
  • • Interest is high even though current service usage is low: 54.9% are very expectant and 76.47% are willing or very willing to use a guide in the future.
  • • Their top needs are stronger interactivity, richer explanation content, and fast mobile access that fits into fragmented routines.

Design priority: useful first, then playful and immersive

Campus Visitors

A smaller data voice, but a core product audience

  • • Visitors are not directly represented as heavily as students, so this segment is built from the project brief plus first-use signals in the questionnaire.
  • • 50.98% prefer 3D/VR panoramic tours when no human guide is available, which aligns closely with visitor orientation and discovery needs.
  • • 52.94% still want very fast loading and simple entry points, showing that immersive content only works if access remains low-friction.

Design priority: reassurance, landmark recognition, and memorable first impressions

3 Must-Have Requirements

Based on the personas, benchmark review, and survey results, the system needs three non-negotiable qualities. These are the minimum requirements for X·EasyGo to feel useful, believable, and worth returning to.

Requirement 01

Instant route clarity

The platform must show the next useful step immediately, including destination name, route direction, and recognisable landmarks. Users should be able to orient themselves without opening multiple apps or decoding campus abbreviations first.

Why it matters: both students and visitors need confidence before they will engage any deeper content.

Requirement 02

Layered content with low friction

Information should arrive in layers: quick map-like guidance first, then optional deeper explanation through XR storytelling, service information, or AI-supported answers. This keeps the interface fast for urgent use while still allowing meaningful exploration.

Why it matters: users want richer content, but not at the cost of speed or readability.

Requirement 03

Playful interaction tied to place

Check-ins, XR moments, and lightweight rewards should be connected to real campus locations and real learning value. Playfulness should encourage movement, curiosity, and memory, rather than turning the experience into a detached game layer.

Why it matters: engagement is strongest when interaction feels meaningful to the campus itself.

Evidence of Life

Our evidence-of-life material focuses on the real situations the design needs to respond to on campus: long walking routes, moments of hesitation at decision points, highly distinctive buildings that could support storytelling, and the transition between academic, social, and service spaces.

What we observed on site

  • • Key routes often contain several decision points where users need reassurance that they are still moving in the right direction.
  • • Some campus buildings have strong visual identity, but their function is not always obvious to a first-time visitor.
  • • Everyday student movement is task-driven, so navigation support needs to be fast and interruption-free.
  • • The campus also has spaces with strong atmosphere and symbolic value, which are suitable for XR storytelling and playful check-ins.

What this means for the design

  • • Routes should be guided through clear landmark cues rather than relying only on building codes or abstract labels.
  • • Important spaces need short, readable introductions that explain both function and educational meaning.
  • • Playful features should appear at memorable places where interaction strengthens orientation and recall.
  • • The final experience should support two rhythms at once: efficient movement for students and exploratory discovery for visitors.
